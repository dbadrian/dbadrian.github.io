Introduction

Note: If you are more interested in the purely scientific version of this article, check out our paper on arvix!

When I started my Master's Thesis my topic was as general as possible: Find something cool (=publishable) with regards to Knowledge Graphs (KG). Having zero previous experience with the topic (similarly for my supervisors), I had a challenging start of reading up the last few years of research.

Very quickly I realized that a bulk of recent research concentrated on modifying well established models, in particular TransE (a very simple and constrained, but surprisingly good model inspired by Word2Vec).

However, most of the results are rather disappointing to say the least. Many of the results barely improve upon the base models (<5% improvement), while sacrificing linear space- and computational complexity and ending up with quadratic or worse.

Yet, some models are quite exciting and make up for it. In the space of latent distance and factorization models this includes, in my opinion, TorusE (TransE on a... torus - it actually does resolve some very annoying constraints of TransE), DistMult (for the sake of its important to ComplEx and my own model, but has been invented in the 70s as INDSCAL), HolE (using circular correlation and making connections to holographic models of associative memory), and ComplEx (a complex-valued version of DistMult). The latter two have been shown to be equivalent, with ComplEx commonly used as reference model as it is the simpler one. And as in every other area, neural networks are on the rise. Two interesting models are R-GCN and ConvE (although the latter is considerably simpler, faster, and better). Some concerns about the implementation and evaluation off the latter was brought up lately (see issue section), but it still seems to be reasonably decent.

Soon (TM)

RMult: A Simple But Universal Factorization Model for Knowledge Graph Completion

So, the question of my thesis can be scoped a little bit. How can I approve upon these models. Faster? Better? Smaller? All?

If I were to make a wish-list, based on the advantages of each model, it boils down to this:

  1. Universal [1] (unlike DistMult or TransE)
  2. No unnecessary computations (unlike ComplEx [2])
  3. Real-valued [3] (unlike ComplEx)
  4. Linear space- and computational complexity (unlike RESCAL)
  5. Parameter efficient [4] (like ConvE)
  6. Achieves state-of-the-art results (like ConvE)
[1]This term is adopted from CITEPAPER, and simply refers to the ability of a model to represent any given KG, given that the embedding dimension is chosen sufficiently large. Both DistMult and TransE do not fulfill that requirement, and these constraints become evident during evaluation.
[2]While ComplEx can represent both symmetric and asymmetric relations, it does so by zeroing out either the real or imaginary part of the relation embedding. Although this seems like a naturally representation, it does carry out several multiplications which do not directly contribute to the final score.
[3]ComplEx is a powerful model by itself, but there is a large body of research concerned with integrating other modalities, such as images or text, to improve the embedding quality. Combination of real-valued and complex-valued systems is not straightforward, so I claim that a purely real-valued model is easier to work with for the time being.
[4]ConvE made strong claims about its parameter efficiency (= number of free parameters required to achieve a certain accuracy). While the bugs found decreases that quality, it still seems considerably more efficient than a standard DistMult and ComplEx.
